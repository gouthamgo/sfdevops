## Total Compensation Breakdown

### Components of Tech Compensation

**1. Base Salary**
```markdown
âœ… Guaranteed cash you receive every paycheck
âœ… Used to calculate 401k match, bonuses (often % of base)
âœ… Most stable component
âŒ Usually smallest component at senior levels

Negotiation tip: This is your floor. Negotiate this first.
```

**2. Signing Bonus**
```markdown
âœ… One-time cash payment (usually in first paycheck)
âœ… Often used to offset equity you're leaving behind
âœ… Highly negotiable
âš ï¸ May have 1-year clawback if you leave

Typical ranges:
- Junior: $10K-$25K
- Mid: $25K-$75K
- Senior: $75K-$200K
- Staff+: $200K-$500K

Negotiation tip: Ask for signing bonus to cover:
- Unvested equity at current company
- Relocation costs
- Gap until first equity vesting
```

**3. Equity / Stock Options**
```markdown
Types:
- RSUs (Restricted Stock Units): Most common at public companies
- Stock Options: Common at startups
- Performance Stock Units (PSUs): Tied to performance metrics

Vesting schedules:
- Standard: 25% per year over 4 years
- Backloaded: 5%, 15%, 40%, 40% (Amazon style)
- Monthly: 1/48th per month over 4 years
- Cliff: Wait 1 year, then 25% vests, rest monthly

â— Key question to ask:
"What's the strike price and current valuation?" (for options)
"How many shares, not dollar value?" (for RSUs)
"What's the vesting schedule?"
"Is there a cliff?"
```

**4. Annual Bonus**
```markdown
Types:
- Performance bonus: Based on your performance (10-20% of base)
- Company bonus: Based on company performance (5-15% of base)
- Guaranteed first year: Some companies guarantee Year 1 bonus

Example (E4 at Google):
- Target: 15% of base
- Base: $180K
- Target bonus: $27K
- Actual: Can be 0% to 200% of target ($0-$54K)

Negotiation tip: Ask about target and historical payout percentages
```

**5. Equity Refreshers**
```markdown
Annual equity grants to retain you (after initial grant starts vesting out)

Example:
- Year 1: Initial grant vesting (25%)
- Year 2: Initial (25%) + Refresher 1 (25%)
- Year 3: Initial (25%) + Refresher 1 (25%) + Refresher 2 (25%)
- Year 4: Initial (25%) + Refresher 1 (25%) + Refresher 2 (25%) + Refresher 3 (25%)

This creates the "golden handcuffs" - your comp goes UP over time.

Negotiation tip: Ask about typical refresher amounts during negotiation
```

**6. Benefits**
```markdown
Healthcare:
- Medical, dental, vision (usually free or subsidized)
- HSA contributions: $1K-$3K per year
- Life insurance, disability

Perks:
- 401k match: 3-6% (some companies like Meta: 50% match on first 6%)
- Commuter benefits: $300/month
- Wellness stipend: $100-$500/month
- Learning budget: $1K-$5K/year
- Equipment: $3K+ (laptop, monitors, chair, desk)

Total value: $10K-$30K per year

Negotiation tip: Less negotiable, but ask about relocation, remote work
```

## Advanced Negotiation Tactics

### 1. The Competing Offer Strategy

**Most Powerful Tool**: Having multiple offers

```markdown
Scenario: You have 2-3 offers

Step 1: Get all offers in writing
Step 2: Create comparison spreadsheet
Step 3: Identify your top choice
Step 4: Use other offers to negotiate with top choice

Example:
"I have an offer from [Company X] at $320K total comp.
I prefer your role because [reasons], but the compensation
gap is significant. Is there any way to get closer?"

Results:
- 70% of the time: They improve the offer
- 20% of the time: They don't budge
- 10% of the time: They rescind (rare, and you have backup)
```

**What if you don't have competing offers?**

Option 1: Apply to more companies simultaneously
Option 2: Use market data instead
```
"Based on levels.fyi and conversations with my network,
L5 DevOps Engineers at similar companies are seeing $320-360K.
Given my experience with [rare skill], I was hoping to be in
that range."
```

### 2. The "I'm Leaving Money on the Table" Tactic

```markdown
Calculate your unvested equity:

Current Company Equity:
- Remaining RSUs: 500 shares
- Current stock price: $200
- Unvested value: $100,000

Ask for signing bonus to cover:
"I'm leaving $100K in unvested equity. Would you be able to
offer a signing bonus to offset that?"

This works because:
âœ… It's objective (you ARE leaving money)
âœ… It's a one-time ask (signing bonus doesn't affect future budgets)
âœ… It's fair (you're not asking for more, just replacement)
```

### 3. The Level Bump Strategy

```markdown
Scenario: Offered L4, but you think you're L5

Research:
- What's the difference in scope?
- What's the TC difference? (usually $50-100K)
- Do you meet L5 criteria?

Approach:
"I'm excited about the offer. Based on my [X years experience]
and [specific achievements at scale], I was expecting to come
in at L5. Can we discuss what it would take to be leveled at
L5, or what the performance expectations would be for a 6-month
promotion to L5?"

Results:
- Best case: Bumped to L5 immediately (+$50-100K)
- Middle case: Written 6-month promotion plan
- Worst case: Clear gap analysis for future promotion
```

### 4. The Equity Refresher Guarantee

```markdown
Problem: Initial grant is great, but what about years 3-4
when it vests out?

Ask:
"Can you share what typical refresher grants look like for
this level? I want to understand the long-term compensation
trajectory."

Better ask:
"Would it be possible to include a minimum refresher grant
guarantee in my offer letter for year 2?"

Why this works:
- Shows you're thinking long-term
- Very few candidates ask this
- Demonstrates understanding of equity compensation
```

### 5. The Remote Work Premium

```markdown
Scenario: They want you in office, you want remote

Option A: Negotiate remote + lower salary
Option B: Negotiate remote + same salary
Option C: Negotiate hybrid + same salary

Approach:
"I'm most productive working remotely, which I've demonstrated
at my current role. I understand there may be a salary
adjustment for remote positions. What's the comp difference
between on-site and remote for this role?"

Then negotiate:
"Given that I'll save the company [office costs / relocation],
would you consider keeping the compensation the same for remote?"
```

## Negotiation Scripts by Scenario

### Scenario 1: Startup Offer vs FAANG Offer

```markdown
Startup Offer:
- Base: $150K
- Equity: 0.5% (potentially worth $0-$5M+ if IPO)
- Signing: $20K
- Current valuation: $200M
- TC (today): $220K
- TC (if $1B exit): $5.22M

FAANG Offer:
- Base: $180K
- Equity: $400K/4 years = $100K/year
- Signing: $40K
- Bonus: $27K
- TC (Year 1): $347K
- TC (4 years): $1.12M

Your script:
"[Startup], I have a FAANG offer at $347K in Year 1. I'm
excited about your mission and growth potential, but the
guaranteed compensation gap is significant. Can we discuss
either:
1. Increasing equity to [0.75-1%], or
2. Increasing base to [$170K], or
3. Larger signing bonus [$50K] to offset the risk?"
```

### Scenario 2: Internal Transfer Negotiation

```markdown
Current role: DevOps Engineer (L4)
New role: Senior DevOps Engineer (L5) on different team

Mistake: Assuming internal transfers don't get raises

Correct approach:
"I'm excited about the L5 opportunity. What's the compensation
package for this role? I want to make sure it aligns with
external L5 market rates [$320K-$360K total comp]."

Why: Internal transfers often CAN negotiate, especially for
level changes. Don't leave money on the table!
```

### Scenario 3: Negotiating After a Lowball Offer

```markdown
Expected: $250K TC
Received: $180K TC

Don't: Get angry or emotional

Do:
"Thanks for the offer. I want to be transparent - based on
[market data / my experience / competing offers], I was
expecting total compensation in the $240-270K range. The
current offer is significantly below that. Is there substantial
room to move on the compensation package?"

If they say no or minimal movement:
"I appreciate your time, but this gap is too large for me to
bridge. If circumstances change or you're able to go back to
the team, I'd be happy to reconsider."

Then: Walk away. Lowball offers often mean they'll undervalue
you throughout your tenure.
```
